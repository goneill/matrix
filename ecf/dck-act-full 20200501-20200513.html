MEMO ENDORSEMENT as to Emanuel Falconer on 247 LETTER by Emanuel Falconer addressed to Judge Valerie E. Caproni from Emanuel Falconer, dated 4/22/2020, re: Compassionate Release. ENDORSEMENT: The Court views Mr. Falconer's request as a motion for a sentence reduction pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1). Mr. Falconer's efforts towards obtaining a GED and his participation in BOP programs are commendable, and the Court continues to encourage those positive steps. Mr. Falconer's ability to remain incident-free is also worthy of praise. Similarly, the Court is sympathetic to Mr. Falconer's desire to be with his family during these times. Nevertheless, the Court's authority to grant relief pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1) is narrow. First, Mr. Falconer must exhaust his administrative remedies by making a request to BOP for compassionate release before making an application to the Court; the exhaustion requirement is satisfied if BOP fails to take action within 30 days of the request. See 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1) (a). Even assuming that the exhaustion requirement can be waived, the circumstances in the letter are not sufficiently exigent as to justify a waiver of a statutory requirement that expires within 30 days. Finally, relief can only be granted pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1) if Mr. Falconer identifies "extraordinary and compelling reasons." Mr. Falconer's present circumstances do not constitute "extraordinary" reasons because he does not face a higher risk of contracting COVID-19 compared to other inmates, and his separation from his loved ones is a reality shared by virtually all other prisoners. Mr. Falconer's request for a sentencing reduction must therefore be denied. A copy of this Order has been mailed to Mr. Falconer by chambers staff. SO ORDERED. (Signed by Judge Valerie E. Caproni on 5/1/2020) (lnl)

ORDER as to Justin Laurel: On April 29, 2020, defendant Justin Laurel filed a motion for "compassionate release" pursuant to 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(A)(i). That statute contains an explicit exhaustion requirement: "[T]he court, upon motion of the Director of the Bureau of Prisons, or upon motion of the defendant after the defendant has fully exhausted all administrative rights to appeal a failure of the Bureau of Prisons to bring a motion on the defendant's behalf or the lapse of 30 days from the receipt of such a request by the warden of the defendants facility, whichever is earlier, may reduce the term of imprisonment... if it finds that... extraordinary and compelling reasons warrant such a reduction." Defendant concedes he did not fully exhaust his administrative remedies under Section 3582 prior to filing the instant motion. The Court does not have the power to waive that requirement. See, e.g., United States v. Roberts, 2020 WL 1700032 (S.D.N.Y. Apr. 8, 2020). By May 8, 2020, the government shall advise the Court in writing whether it is willing to waive the exhaustion requirement in this case. If the government is not willing to waive, the Court will deny the instant motion without prejudice to re-filing after the exhaustion requirement has been satisfied. Chambers will mail a copy of this Order to defendant at the following address: Justin Laurel Reg. No. 72115-054 LSCI Butner P.O. Box 999 Butner, NC 27509. SO ORDERED. (Signed by Judge Vincent L. OPINION & ORDER as to (86-Cr-1124-4) Enrique Rivera. Before the Court is an emergency motion by Defendant Enrique Rivera a/k/a Eulogio Portes ("Portes") for a sentence reduction to time served and his immediate release from the Metropolitan Detention Center, Brooklyn ("the MDC"), or, in the alternative, for immediate transfer to home confinement, due to Portes's advanced age, compromised health, and his status as a medically "high-risk" inmate who is especially vulnerable to contracting the Coronavirus, COVID-19 ("COVID-19"). Portes brings the motion pursuant to the First Step Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(A) ("the Act"), commonly known as the compassionate release statute. The Government opposes Portes's motion as procedurally barred because he did not satisfy the Act's administrative exhaustion requirements before seeking judicial intervention, and substantively meritless because Portes's preexisting medical conditions, while serious, do not justify a reduction to the already lenient sentence the Court imposed less than three months ago. For the reasons set forth below, the Court finds that Portes is not entitled to relief pursuant to the Act because the provisions of 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c), which became effective on November 1, 1987, do not apply to his term of incarceration, which was imposed for criminal conduct that occurred prior to that date. However, Portes's motion is GRANTED pursuant to Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 35(b), in effect prior to amendment by Public Law 98-473 and applicable to offenses committed prior to November 1, 1987. Accordingly, Portes is to be released from the MDC tomorrow, May 2, 2020, and he is to immediately begin serving the remaining 138 days of his original sentence in home confinement, after first undergoing a 14-day quarantine, both of which must take place at the locations designated in Portes's April 23, 2020 letter to the Court. (See Letter from John M. Hillebrecht, DLA Piper LLP (US), to Hon. John F. Keenan (Apr. 23, 2020), ECF No. 72.)...[See this Opinion & Order]... III. Order: In view of the above, it is hereby ORDERED that Enrique Rivera a/k/a Eulogio Portes, Register Number 17330-054, is RESENTENCED to TIME SERVED plus 138 days of probation, which must be served in home confinement and monitored by such technology and on such conditions as the U.S. Probation Department for the Southern District of New York deems appropriate. It is FURTHER ORDERED that the following additional terms of Portes's probation are hereby imposed: 1. Portes must call his Probation Officer the day he is released, and he must strictly follow any instructions he receives at that time or in subsequent conversations. 2. Portes must call the New York State 24-Hour Coronavirus Hotline (1-888-364-3065) the day he is released, and he must strictly follow the medical and social distancing advice he receives. 3.Portes must strictly self-isolate from others at the Haverhill, Massachusetts location designated in his April 23, 2020 letter, (ECF No. 72), until at least May 17, 2020. 4.Immediately upon completion of the 14-day quarantine,Portes must reside at the Lynn, Massachusetts location designated in his April 23, 2020 letter, (ECF No. 72), until at least September 17, 2020. It is FURTHER ORDERED that Enrique Rivera a/k/a Eulogio Portes, Register Number 17330-054, be released from the MDC tomorrow, May 2, 2020. IV. Conclusion: For the reasons set forth above, Portes's motion for reduction in sentence or home confinement pursuant to the First Step Act, 18 U.S.C. § 3582(c)(1)(A), is DENIED. However, Portes's motion is GRANTED pursuant to former Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 35(b). SO ORDERED. (Signed by Judge John F. Keenan on 5/1/2020)(bw)

ORDER denying 272 Motion to Reduce Sentence as to Antonious Samuel (3): Under 18 USC 3582(c)(1)(a), I may, after considering the factors set forth in 18 USC 3553(a), reduce a sentence if extraordinary and compelling reasons justify such action and it is consistent with the relevant policy statements of the Sentencing Commission. Policy Statement 1B1.13 imposes similar requirements, along with the provision that the Defendant not be a danger to the safety of any other person or the community. Application Note 1 to Policy Statement 1B1.13 describes four potential extraordinary and compelling reasons: 1) the defendant has a terminal medical condition or because of such a health condition is substantially diminished in his ability to provide self-care; 2) the defendant is at least 65 years old and has served 75% of his sentence; 3) family circumstances; and 4) an extraordinary and compelling reason other than or in combination with one of the above. It is not clear whether Mr. Samuels has exhausted his administrative remedies. He says he applied for compassionate release on 4/4/20 and thus thirty days have run. The Bureau of Prisons ("BOP") says he did not apply until 4/22/20. I will assume for the sake of argument that Mr. Samuels has exhausted. Defendant suggests that he meets the fourth category listed above (extraordinary and compelling reason other than or in combination with one listed) because he has two medical conditions -- asthma and obesity -- that put him at increased risk should he be stricken with COVID-19. While his asthma appears to be mild and well controlled, given that his medical records apparently reflect that he rarely uses his inhaler, I will assume that the combination of factors he posits do put him at increased risk for serious illness should he contract the virus. The risk to him is less extraordinary and compelling than it would be were he housed at a different facility, because it seems there are no staff or inmate cases of the virus at FCI Schuylkill, although that could of course change. In any event, I still must balance the extraordinary and compelling reason Mr. Samuels posits against the factors set forth in 18 USC 3553(a). See USA v. Daugerdas, 2020 WL 2097653 (S.D.N.Y. 5/1/20). Those factors weigh against release. Mr. Samuels was sentenced to 60-months for managing a high-end cocaine operation, a role he took over after the original leader was charged with federal narcotics offenses. Mr. Samuels not only ran the business -- including obtaining supply, hiring workers and arranging sales -- but he funneled proceeds to his predecessor. His sentence of 60 months was appropriate, considering the nature and circumstances of the offense, the history and characteristics of the Defendant, and the other 3553(a) factors. It would undermine several of those factors to release Mr. Samuels now, when he has served only about one-third of his sentence. Such a substantial reduction would have Mr. Samuels serving a sentence that is not just punishment. It would diminish respect for the law for an offender to get such a large reduction essentially because he is obese, and it would introduce unwarranted sentencing disparity. See Daugerdas. The Court believes, however, that Mr. Samuels' health could be safeguarded without undermining the 3553(a) factors by a temporary furlough under 18 USC 3622. Only BOP can order such a furlough, but the Court recommends that BOP give serious consideration to temporary release for Mr. Samuels, under proper conditions, after which he would complete his sentence. While Mr. Samuels' continuing the cocaine business after his boss was charged suggests he could be a danger to the community, he did not revert to criminal conduct while on pretrial release and the Court believes that he does not otherwise present a danger. (The Court notes that the community to which Mr. Samuels would be released -- Yonkers, New York -- has experienced a high level of corona virus infections, so suitable quarantine arrangements would have to be made.) A temporary furlough, rather than a sentence reduction, strikes the Court as the most fair and sensible balance of both the public interest and the risk to Mr. Samuels. The Government is directed to send a copy of this text order to the Warden and the legal department at FCI Schuylkill.. (Signed by Judge Cathy Seibel on 05/05/2020) (Seibel, Cathy)
